But, you may say, you were going to speak about science and higher education. What does that have to do with ‘room for God and others’? I will try to explain.

When I received your text message suggesting ‘Science and ____ity’ as the title of my talk, I put calculations aside and reclined my office chair. I thought about the proposed title, how to fill the blank, and the meaning of the word *science*.

Science might mean *technoscience* – science and technology applied to the control of nature, perhaps even human nature, for better or for worse. Or science might mean pursuing *pure* or *basic science* to understand nature. As in this sentence written a half-century ago:

> *What the world needs now is a new kind of Pythagorean science, not technologies that move in utter contempt for the ecology that supports human life*.

In the basement of the Integrated Science Center, my office window looks out to a brick embankment. Do I want to talk about pure and basic science – or technology and applied science – in the narrow sense of the positivist natural sciences (physics, chemistry, biology)? Or in a broader sense that includes the human sciences (anthropology, sociology, economics)?

If science means technoscience, its broader sense encompasses social and political *technics*, the applied behavioral sciences, managerial strategies, the necessity of propaganda, surveillance technologies, and so on. I felt uneasy because a monologue about *technological society* – if the blank were filled that way – would be the speculations of a layman. My colleagues would smile in their beards and brand me a dilettante, noting my lack of relevant credentials and the amount of time I have for reading. In this way, these symbols of academic derision would deftly diminish both secondary research and work-life balance. Besides – if the suffix was important – the word ‘society’ ended with -ety, not -ity, as in your text.

My legs were stiff. I needed perspective. I walked out of the ISC toward Old Campus with a sweater, snack, and cell phone. Passing Crim Dell bridge, I thought, science can even mean, though I doubted you meant it to mean, ‘any domain of systematic knowledge amenable to reason.’ In this classical sense, the Sciences include what we now call the Humanities. As in: *Our medieval ancestors considered theology to be ‘Queen of the Sciences.’* Or: *In the College of Arts & Sciences, email signatures end with a list of pronouns such as she/her/hers.* The broader interpretation – science meaning all quests for publicly validated truth – would anchor the lecture in my life story and the concerns of a man ensconced in middle academia.

I traversed a brick path that crossed the Sunken Garden from McGlothlin-Street Hall (geology, computer science) to James Blair (history, philosophy). ‘Science and *History*’? Walking through Old Campus always reminds me that the past informs the present. ‘Science and *Philosophy*’? Analytic (language and logic) or Continental (the phenomenology of The Things Themselves)? *Science and Humanities*? Questions like: How do college students experience the broad academic categories that partition professors? What are their relative merits? What is the theoretical minimum of critical thought (as opposed to problem-solving) in a liberal S.T.E.M. (science, technology, engineering, mathematics) curriculum? Or the obverse – an educator’s perspective on intellectual specialization à la C.P. Snow. I have not read his novels, but his lament pierces my heart. At a university, no research scientist *here* understands what the literary types do *over there*, though we have our doubts and suspicions.

The sociology of knowledge intrigues me, I thought, and academic snobbery *is* amusing. ‘We have results, they have opinions.’ A modicum of library research might suffice if I focused on my trans-disciplinarity. But – the astute reader will note – like *society* before, neither *philosophy* nor *history* ended in ‘ity.’ *Humanities* did not even rhyme.

Chancellors Hall (next to Blair) contains Economics, Government, International Relations, and Public Policy. The faculty who identify as social scientists, as do many in the Schools of Business and Education, occupy the middle ground of the Arts & Sciences taxonomy at William & Mary,

1.  Arts, Letters, and Values (ALV)

2.  Culture, Society, and the Individual (CSI)

3.  The Natural World and Quantitative Reasoning (NQR).

Anthropology, linguistics, and other human sciences transcend or integrate these ‘knowledge domains.’ The NQR disciplines are traditionally ordered from ‘hard science’ to ‘soft science’ (Mathematics &gt; Physics &gt; Chemistry &gt; Biology) based on the quantitative reasoning required. Of course, it is problematic to use gendered adjectives like ‘hard’ and ‘soft’ in this way. For one thing, the ranking ‘hard to soft’ can be a cipher for ‘difficult to easy,’ or ‘tit’ as we used to say in college.

I first learned that science and technology were gendered while applying to university. Four decades later, I still own *At the Edge of History: Speculations on the Transformation of Culture*, a paperback lent to me by a mentor and graduate of Yale. In Chapter 3, William Irwin Thompson, formerly a humanities instructor at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, argued that *what distinguished M.I.T. from any other university was ... its overwhelming lust for power*. This is how I acquired – 40 years ago – the above-mentioned distinction between science (truth) and technology (power). I internalized this division and its corollary: *the expressive nature of education and the repressive nature of training*, that is, the dichotomy of thought and lobotomy. The highlighted passages within *Speculations* reveal the concerns of an adolescent male deciding whether to attend a technical university or a liberal arts college.

> *Every humanist knows that sex and death make us what we are...*

> *The sexual mythology of rational male dominance over irrational female nature...*

> *...the diffusely sensual and androgynous quality of sexuality in the emerging \[1970s\] post-childbearing era. Breast and penis become indistinguishable as they become one oral gratification. As the breast is turned into an intrusive phallus, and the daggerlike phallus of the old male is turned into a lactating breast, the ancient roles of Mother and Father disappear and ‘man’ is succeeded by the androgynous angel or demon. These new bisexual mutants...*

At the time, I did not understand the Freudian language (oral versus genital gratification), but you see what I mean about the two academic cultures and not understanding what they do over there.

Today, *At the Edge of History* reads as a delightful combination of posh and prescience. Some speculations are as ridiculous as the ‘unsolved historical mysteries’ of Erich von Däniken’s *Chariots of the Gods?*. And yet – I found Thompson’s description of M.I.T.’s imposing architectural style to be accurate. The *colossus* of the Earth Sciences Building – the *monumentality* of Killian Court – the *imperial* marble buildings, Roman columns, and Pantheonic dome – the friezes carved in large Latin letters: Aristotle, Archimedes, Copernic<u>v</u>s, Paste<u>v</u>r, and others – a chorus of scientific ancestors urging us to worship the Institute *as a god who bestows technologies on modern civilization*.

This raises a question. Informed as I was about the emerging androgynous quality of sexuality, why did I attend M.I.T.? The short answer is that Father and Mother expected me to be an engineer. After much consternation and family drama – exacerbated by a looming divorce and parental skepticism about liberal arts education – I conceded that the Institute would work for me. Within a year, I informed my parents that (1) I preferred recombinant DNA to semi-conductors and (2) *Modern Poetry* was surprisingly rigorous.

I pledged to a fraternity located in Boston’s Back Bay. At night we ‘tooled’ (studied) and looked across the Charles River to the ‘The Great Breast of Knowledge’ (the Institute Pantheon). During the day, I meandered through the *interminable gray corridors* of Building 10 – past photographs by Harold Edgerton, the father of modern high-speed photography, and a portrait of Norbert Wiener, the father of cybernetics – searching for meaning within a rigorous technical education.

<hr class="ornament">

Chancellors Hall (formerly Tyler Hall) is adjacent to James Blair. The Department of Government was quick to ‘revisit whom and what we commemorate’ when the opportunity arose in 2021. Although there was *some ambiguity about exactly who the building was named for*: the 10th president of the United States or the 17th president of William & Mary, neither the father – *enslaver, secessionist, and Confederate representative* – or son – *author of a pamphlet that remains popular among white supremacist groups* – was fostering an inclusive campus where all feel welcome and valued. Why the campus welcomed Henry Kissinger as Chancellor (2000–2005) is a question I asked before accepting a tenure-track position at William & Mary in 2001.

Next is Tucker Hall, the home of English, Linguistics, and Medieval & Renaissance Studies. Adjunct writing and modern language instructors orbit the building obliquely like dwarf planets. Then comes the namesake of Sir Christopher Wren, *the oldest college building still standing in the United States*, and ‘the soul of the College’ for more than three centuries. The second and third floors of Wren are devoted to religious studies – the study of world religions as socio-cultural phenomena irrespective of whether the beliefs themselves are true – which is a variety of academic freedom if not religious experience. The Wren Building *has been gutted by fire three times – in 1705, 1859 and 1862* – as though the College’s soul required purification by fire.

The first floor of Wren is open to the public. In the north wing is a refectory known as the Great Hall. Famous paintings of King William and Queen Mary overlook the fireplace and unfinished wood floors. The Wren Chapel – where I was married in 2015 – was added as a south wing in 1732. Exiting Wren, I walked past bronze Lord Botetourt on the wedge-shaped lawn flanked by the President’s House and the Brafferton. I had reached the origin of the campus in polar coordinates, known as ‘Confusion Corner’ due to the confluence of vehicular and pedestrian traffic. The Latin words for the curriculum of medieval universities – *trivium* (grammar, rhetoric, logic) and *quadrivium* (arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, music) – literally mean ‘place where three or four roads meet.’ Crossing the walkway to Colonial Williamsburg, I felt closer to the sought-after title for my talk, as though insights were lurking nearby.

I walked the center of Duke of Gloucester Street toward Bruton Parish Episcopal Church. Science and *Trinity*? *Divinity*? A historic site open to visitors was marked with a Continental Flag (a Union Jack canton and a field of 13 red and white stripes). As I passed roadblocks forbidding motor vehicles, the inevitable anachronisms accumulated. The blue sky was geodesically striped with water vapor nucleated by jet exhaust. An interracial couple enjoyed a leisurely ride by horse-drawn carriage. The ears pierced with airpods harked back to an ancient custom of servanthood.

The Governor’s Palace, *home to seven royal governors and Virginia’s first two elected governors*, was built to impress. *Authority?* *Prosperity?* It was reconstructed in the 1930s – as was the Colonial Capitol building at the east end of DoG St. – as part of the restoration of Colonial Williamsburg. The Palace Green invites family picnics and children practicing ‘rolling a hoop with a stick.’

Tourists amused themselves with stock and pillory as I passed between the Armory and Courthouse. One loner was publicly humiliated by the impossibility of taking a selfie with head and wrists clamped. From stairs in front of a tavern, an interpreter contrasted colonies.

> *The Pilgrims were dissenters from the Church of England. They came to the New World to practice their religion freely without fear of persecution. Jamestown was established as a profit-making enterprise sponsored by the Virginia Company of London. These colonists were supporters of the Church of England and felt a strong connection to their homeland...*

In summary,

> <u>Plymouth</u>: Religious freedom and conviction, cold rocky soil – lumber, shipbuilding, and fishing.
>
> <u>Jamestown</u>: Free enterprise and profit-making, warm fertile soil – cash, tobacco, and slavery.

Both *Equity* and *Diversity* ended with -ity, but filling the blank this way seemed unjustifiable, unwelcome, and likely to spark a courageous conversation with a dean. Sensitized to these ideals – now captured by bureaucracy and processed into institutional procedures – my reflex was to pass the microphone to a colleague who could speak from an admissible standpoint.

In this imaginary faculty meeting, Nyeki was a tenured professor, an expert on the African-initiated churches. She wore well-fitted black jeans and a vibrantly colored Ankara cape. The Ghana braids that fell from her head ended with flamboyant beads. Her chandelier earrings, she once explained, are Gye Nyame, meaning “fear nothing except for God.” Nyeki looked at me through intelligent green-amber eyes. With a bangle-clad wrist, she gestured toward the words being projected at the front of the chamber. The faculty were discussing the College Curriculum. Specifically, the need for new courses that consider *difference, equity, and justice in contemporary life in the United States*,

> *integrating consideration of social norms, institutional practices, and patterns of belonging and marginalization through the lens of race and at least one other social identity.*

In theory, these courses were offered from disciplines throughout Arts & Sciences, but everyone knew it was unlikely that a class of this type was taught by someone like me – a professor of mathematical sciences, I mean. This ‘science problem’ would be explored by a subcommittee provided an NQR faculty member volunteered to serve as chair ... one well-versed in qualitative as well as quantitative research methods ... so as not to overburden ... preferably *not* representative of an underrepresented ... in this important work ...

This is how the imaginary microphone found its way back to my cis straight white male hand that was numbing from nerves. Would it be worthwhile? I *could* stand and speak from the heart without rhetorical skill and oblivious to realpolitik. I wondered, would I dare? *Do* I dare? I cannot orate in complete paragraphs like my ALV colleagues. My scholarship does not present ‘imaginary gardens with real toads in them.’ If I rose to speak in this fictional faculty meeting, would a factual toad hop off my tongue? After presuming to speak and being misunderstood, I will sit back down, embarrassed like a failed toastmaster. The perpetual discourse on curriculum will continue. I will stare out the window, wishing I were colorful like the leaves of Fall, thinking, ‘That’s not it at all. That’s not what I meant, at all.’

The M.I.T. course catalog had options ranging from applied technology to basic science. From hard to soft, my first-semester coursework included: calculus, physics, organic chemistry, *Intro to Life Science* (molecular biology), and *Reading and Writing the Essay*.

*A Room of One’s Own* by Virginia Woolf was a required purchase. The classroom was hidden in an offshoot of the *interminable grey corridors* in the basement of Building 24. This dilapidated structure was rumored to contain the office of Noam Chomsky, who at that time was writing about imperial democracy and CIA intervention in Central America. The adjunct professor of humanities who taught *The Essay* worked for the New York Times Book Review. His curly hair, polo shirt, and blazer with an upturned collar reminded me of friends who had (unlike me) attended private school. The class was intimate; six of ten students confessed to resenting the humanities requirement.

We learned that an ‘essay’ is a short piece of persuasive writing and also a ‘try.’ *A Room of One’s Own* by Virginia Woolf is a narrative essay that makes an argument by telling a story. Its thesis is defended through a stream-of-consciousness monologue, a narrative technique pioneered by Woolf. Her thesis is that an ‘undistracted mind’ is required to write fiction. For a woman to write fiction, she needs money, time, and a room of her own, not a sitting room overflowing with family obligations. The argument resonates with the academic experience of all genders.

I caught the symbolism of the tailless cat. I was intrigued by the concept of an androgynous mind whose creativity emerged from mixing masculine and feminine qualities. The class discovered – or we were led to believe that we had discovered – that the chapters of *A Room* followed the scientific method. Chapter 1 asked the question, ‘Why don’t women write fiction?’

> 1\. Statement of problem (women/fiction)

The remaining chapters were interpreted as follows.

> 2\. Hypothesis\
> 3. Past testing (Shakespeare’s sister)\
> 4. Present testing\
> 5. Conclusion (peroration/you)

*Peroration* meant ‘closing remarks with exhortation.’ The class speculated that the essay, a form of literature that emerged during the Renaissance, was inclined to Enlightenment thinking. We questioned the status of a scientific argument that relied on fictional hypothesis testing and put forward criteria for *truthful* composition.

The most memorable aspects of *The Essay* were the class discussions and a modestly dressed, attractive young woman. Hannah became the focus of controversy in her defense of traditional ideas about wives and husbands. I disagreed with Hannah but gallantly defended the legitimacy of her faith-based complementarian position that *men and women are equal in personhood, but created for different roles*. The other students, male and female, straight and gay, were flabbergasted. Hannah invited me to a Bible study. Her friends insisted that science generally enabled a deeper appreciation of creation, but biological evolution left no ‘room for God.’ Back in class, I discerned that gender roles per se were not the problem. As Woolf said,

> *It would be a thousand pities if women wrote like men, or lived like men, or looked like men, for if two sexes are quite inadequate, considering the vastness and variety of the world, how should we manage with one only? Ought not education to bring out and fortify the differences rather than the similarities?*

What was out of bounds was the origin of Hannah’s opinion in religious conviction. Parsing what mutual submission might mean in a happy marriage with freely chosen gender roles was of no interest to Hannah’s adversaries. Nor were they concerned that an intelligent female mechanical engineer in training was – if she found a husband – planning to put her career on hold and be a stay-at-home mother through preschool. What was problematic was that *she saw this as God’s plan for her life*. The pluralism of that classroom was contingent on religion having been scrubbed off social identity. Our engagement with value-laden topics had no ‘room for God.’

My criticism of Hannah – never expressed – began when her religious conviction invalidated the differences among our student colleagues. Her belief in God seemed to require the exclusion of other people, for example, Chloe and Olivia, precisely because they liked each other. *Let us admit ... that these things sometimes happen.* Certain people are not welcome in certain churches.

I must have mentioned *The Essay* to Nyeki. As the faculty discussion continued, my attention sank to a journal article on my clipboard. The College Curriculum needed *science* courses that addressed difference, equity, and justice. I did not presume to speak, but Nyeki leaned over to pen this suggestion in the margin of the article:

> God & Others.\
> A Room for—?\
> Make Room for—?

She might have written *Theol<u>ogy</u>* or *Alter<u>ity</u>*, but had not. Confused, I took out my phone and reread your text proposing ‘Science and ____ity’ as the title of my talk. I thumbed my reply:

> About title .. does suffix matter?

Your answer:

> In Aquinas, theology is queen  
> Science and whatever  
> ITY = I Trust You :-)

I will spare you from self-deprecating humor about the ridiculousness of designing a lecture around a title with a final word ending in I, T, and Y. As I recall, the faculty easily agreed on most of the College Curriculum (e.g., freshman seminars and capstone courses). Concerns over the difference/equity/justice component were expressed by NQR faculty. In science pedagogy, content exposition takes precedence over interpretation and critique. Math and science courses are cumulative and taken in sequence. Instructors struggle to integrate math and coding in soft sciences that are becoming more quantitative. Although we can envision compelling courses on scientific racism, gender in medicine, and so on, these topics are outside our wheelhouse.

On a memorable Wednesday in 2016, I stood before 70 neuroscience majors. ‘I am not saying there was only one person to vote for. There were two candidates. But I must acknowledge the horrid language used in the recent election. Racism and misogyny are real and powerful forces; some would say demonic.’ I spoke of double standards (and worse) faced by female academic colleagues. I shared my multi-racial family’s experience of privilege, profiling, and police officers with drawn guns. A student thanked me after class. She said that many of her white peers have trouble grasping the concerns of racial minorities and the divergent experience of life with brown skin. She commented that humanities professors were making such comments across campus, but few science professors had done so.

Are science professors less likely than humanities professors to censure demagoguery? If so, why? These are not the types of questions I am trained to answer. But, if the defense of democracy is outside the wheelhouse of NQR faculty, the College Curriculum’s ‘science problem’ reflects a deeper problem. Whatever the relative merits of humanistic education and scientific training, technical proficiency is not an escape clause to the question, ‘How shall we live?’

Years ago, through an exchange program between M.I.T. and Harvard, I took a course on Erik Erickson’s developmental psychoanalysis. He was not responsible for my S.T.E.M. vs. humanities ‘identity crisis,’ but he did coin the term. Harvard students were urban and cosmopolitan compared to rural and lowbrow Institute frat boys. On a double date with my roommate and two Harvard girls, they spoke of their fathers’ yachts. Arguably, they said, the world is run by politicians and lawyers who attended Ivy League schools and majored in humanities. Any techie faithful to their first love was destined to become a well-trained nebbish paid to design devices whose purpose and deployment would be determined by financiers and the educated political class.

The Institute professors knew that education and training are distinct. Jerome Y. Lettvin, a father of modern neuroscience, expressed these concerns in the mid-20th century.

> *We have done our best to make shops of schools and to make ignoble the goals of art and science.*
>
> *Where ideally the universities ought to be ivory towers in which to dream of remade worlds, now they are, as perhaps they always were, shops for the training of apprentice nebbishes to fit within the existing system.*
>
> *The schools have become businesses rather than a refuge from the world, and ideas, as ideals, have become devalued.*

While teaching sensory neuroscience, Jerry Lettvin described the experience of an auditory hallucination. He ‘heard’ a clarinet playing in the distance. As the melody repeated, he made for the kitchen and turned the sink faucet to a slow drip. Try as he might, it proved impossible to synchronize the makeshift metronome to the time signature of the melody. This experience convinced him that ‘hearing’ does not occur sequentially in time. A ‘seeing’ may also have this quality of time out of joint. Past, present, and future – or distinct places and settings – may be exhibited like a short stack of translucent images that cohere and interpret one another.

While I sat on a bench near the Colonial Capitol enjoying mulled cider, a child played with a Colonial-style button buzzer, a threaded disk whirling one way then the other as the strings are pulled. An annoyed cat hopped onto the fence and deftly trotted away. Lettvin spoke of a remarkable creature almost forgotten in the annals of neuroscience: the decerebrate cat. The experiment is no longer necessary or allowed. Anesthetize a cat, open its skull, and carefully remove the cerebrum with an aspirator. Upon recovery, if the ugly underside of the brain is undamaged, the ghostly feline will walk on a treadmill! If startled, the ‘preparation’ will arch its back and hiss. But it is no longer a cat that can hunt butterflies and purr.

I reached the Capitol at the east end of DoG St. speculating that education is subversive because politicians require followers, not citizens. Surrounding the Capitol is a carpet of crushed oyster shells strewn about ‘to be trampled by men.’ *You are the salt of the earth. But if the salt loses its saltiness...* It was high time I returned to my office in the Integrated Science Center. My walk down DoG St. had yielded one needful thing: a title for my talk. *Room for God and Others* (*Make Room—*? *A Room—*?) The first sounded like a peroration. The second conveyed a church or a chapel.

I was startled by the firing of guns. *During the 18th century, guns were customarily fired to salute the Christmas season and as an expression of joy and celebration.* A second shot – and the dissonance of a joyful noise emerging from a weapon of war – transported me to a Grand Illumination. As in years prior, the celebration was inspiring, beautiful, and community-building – but, for me, also dark. Each flaming arrow celebrates victory and freedom, but in reverse color, it also glorifies the violence of war.

I was on the wrong side of the Capitol – too close to the pyrotechnics. As the smoke from the celebration thickened, breathing became difficult. The blasts were concussive and deafening; the flashes a blinding strobe. I scrambled over crushed oyster shells to find shelter, keeping my back to the brick wall surrounding the Capitol. With ash in my eyes, I looked down DoG St. to campus. I could see through the Colonial era to the American Revolution. The Conquest of California. America’s first Civil War. *All dreaded it, all sought to avert it.*

Having found shelter with my back to a barricade, I looked up and suddenly was beneath, not the modest cupola of the colonial Capitol in Williamsburg, but the massive dome of The People’s House. The dome – made of cast iron and painted to look like marble – was completed in 1866, just after the Civil War. The National Mall side of the U.S. Capitol was smoked out. Rioting patriots carried a Gadsden flag over the barricade. Down the Mall, through wafts of tear gas, the blooming cherry trees displayed the strange fruit of national solidarity.

Duke of Gloucester Street was a treadmill carrying Grand Illumination spectators toward Old Campus. The flags that lined the corridor had Confederate cantons with 50 stars. In James Blair, the remaining historians taught the heroic defense of states’ rights against Northern aggression. The economists in Tyler (formerly Chancellors) explained how chattel slavery results in an efficient agrarian economy. In the ‘soul of the College,’ the arguments for white supremacy and Christian theocracy were airtight and Biblical. The Lord Botetourt Statue was stained from a recent sectarian dispute.

The spectators were assembled in the garden between the Great Hall and Wren Chapel, cheering enthusiastically. On a raised platform in front of the Wren was a well-camouflaged but very real toad: the double-horned toad of white Christian nationalism. The toad speaks boastfully; its pale green underside is mottled with bilious orange.

<hr class="ornament">

When my vision cleared, I picked myself up from the white carpet surrounding the Colonial Capitol building. I brushed off crushed oyster shells and walked downhill toward the Public Gaol that, in Colonial times, was a prison for thieves, enslaved runaways, debtors, and political prisoners. The gallows whispered from a hilltop to my right. A gaggle of joggers cascaded past. In addition to death and sex, what makes us what we are?

I sat down on the bank of a small creek that flows past the Gaol. No fish darted about. The spot is near the watershed divide of the James and York Rivers, which meet in a confluence at the Chesapeake Bay. I keep asking that the Left make room for God and the Right make room for Others.

My colleagues with beards are already smiling, amused that someone like me would presume – to pray and to speak – about value-laden topics like gender, race, religion, and the distinction between education and training in a liberal S.T.E.M. curriculum.

<hr class="ornament">

Dear Reader, we will ignore them. We will learn to think for ourselves. We will read, think, discuss, and abide so that together we may...

> *...engage complex, value-laden topics through critical analysis, critical reflection, and respectful dialogue that can bridge differences in perspective.*

‘<u>*Make*</u> room for God and others’ is the greatest commandment of a prominent faith tradition. ‘<u>*A*</u> room for God and others’ is a sanctuary: the classroom. *Discuss*.
